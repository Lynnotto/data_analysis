Ubiquitination in Plasmodium falciparum

Exploring the ubiquitin–proteasome system as a regulatory mechanism and antimalarial drug target.

Introduction

About Plasmodium falciparum

Plasmodium falciparum is the protozoan parasite responsible for the most severe form of human malaria. Transmitted through the bite of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes, it completes a complex life cycle spanning both the mosquito vector and the human host. Within humans, the parasite undergoes asexual replication inside red blood cells — progressing through ring, trophozoite, and schizont stages — before bursting out to infect new cells, causing the cyclical fevers characteristic of the disease.

Malaria caused by P. falciparum remains one of the deadliest infectious diseases globally, claiming hundreds of thousands of lives each year, predominantly in sub-Saharan Africa. The parasite's ability to develop resistance to frontline antimalarials — including artemisinin-based combination therapies — makes the identification of new drug targets an urgent priority.

At the molecular level, P. falciparum relies on tightly regulated post-translational modification pathways to orchestrate stage-specific proteome changes. Among these, the ubiquitin–proteasome system (UPS) has emerged as a central regulatory hub governing parasite development, stress responses, and drug susceptibility.

Research Focus

The Ubiquitin–Proteasome System in Malaria

Ubiquitination is a post-translational modification in which ubiquitin — a small, highly conserved protein — is covalently attached to target proteins. This modification can direct proteins to the proteasome for degradation, alter their activity, or regulate protein–protein interactions. The process is carried out by a cascade of enzymes: E1 activating enzymes, E2 conjugating enzymes, and E3 ubiquitin ligases that confer substrate specificity.

In P. falciparum, the UPS is essential for survival. The parasite encodes multiple E1, E2, and E3 enzymes, as well as deubiquitinating enzymes (DUBs) that reverse ubiquitin modifications. Disruption of key UPS components consistently impairs parasite growth and, in several cases, sensitises parasites to artemisinin — highlighting the UPS as both a biological regulator and a promising therapeutic vulnerability.

A Plasmodium falciparum RING Finger E3 Ubiquitin Ligase Modifies the Roles of PfMDR1 and PfCRT in Parasite Drug Responses

Knockdown of PfRFUL, a RING finger E3 ubiquitin ligase, rendered P. falciparum more sensitive to mefloquine, piperaquine, amodiaquine, and dihydroartemisinin. The study shows that ubiquitination directly regulates key drug transporter proteins PfMDR1 and PfCRT, linking the UPS to antimalarial drug resistance mechanisms.

2023 PubMed

Unusually Divergent Ubiquitin Genes and Proteins in Plasmodium Species

Ubiquitin — among the most conserved proteins in eukaryotes — has undergone unusually rapid sequence divergence in multiple Plasmodium lineages. The RPL40 and RPS27a fusion gene ubiquitins evolved independently in distinct subgenera, and polyubiquitin genes acquired novel codon insertions, revealing lineage-specific evolutionary pressures on the ubiquitin system in malaria parasites.

2017 PubMed

Lys48 Ubiquitination During the Intraerythrocytic Cycle of the Rodent Malaria Parasite, Plasmodium chabaudi

This study maps Lys48-linked polyubiquitination — the canonical proteasomal degradation signal — across ring, trophozoite, and schizont stages of P. chabaudi. Ubiquitinated proteins peak at the schizont stage and correspond to proteins abundant in earlier trophozoite stages, demonstrating that the UPS coordinates protein turnover and cell-cycle progression throughout the intraerythrocytic cycle.

2023 PubMed

A Plasmodium falciparum Ubiquitin-Specific Protease (PfUSP) Is Essential for Parasite Survival and Its Disruption Enhances Artemisinin Efficacy

PfUSP, a deubiquitinating enzyme, is shown to be essential for P. falciparum growth and proteostasis. Knockdown significantly increased parasite sensitivity to dihydroartemisinin without affecting chloroquine susceptibility, indicating that PfUSP helps the parasite tolerate artemisinin-induced cellular stress and represents a target to overcome artemisinin resistance.

DOI: 10.1042/BCJ20220429 View on PubMed
2024 PubMed

Plasmodium falciparum Contains Functional SCF and CRL4 Ubiquitin E3 Ligases, and CRL4 Is Critical for Cell Division and Membrane Integrity

This study characterises two functional Cullin-RING E3 ligase complexes in P. falciparum — PfSCF (Cullin-1) and PfCRL4 (Cullin-2). Depletion of PfCullin-2 severely impaired asexual and sexual erythrocytic development and disrupted protein translation, lipid biosynthesis, and membrane integrity, establishing CRL4 as an essential regulator of parasite cell division and a potential drug target.
